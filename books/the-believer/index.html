The Believer.

David Rourke has pastored First Baptist of Harmon for sixteen years. Three years ago, sitting with a family while their nine-year-old died of leukemia, he prayed and heard only a man talking to a ceiling — and kept talking, because the grieving parents needed the talking. The faith has been gone ever since. A stranger in the back pew tells him what he will say in the Easter sermon to four hundred people: the literal resurrection, the empty tomb, the stone rolled away — and that he won’t believe a word of it. The needing is the thing that keeps him in the pulpit after the belief has left.

The cast.

David Rourke has stood in the pulpit of the white-steepled First Baptist Church on Bishop Hill for sixteen years. The steeple has been the same steeple longer than the faith was ever there. He lost the belief in a hospital room three years ago and has told no one — because telling would be the first domino, and the dominoes go: wife, congregation, job, house, the whole architecture of a life built on a foundation that dissolved.

Sarah Rourke, his wife, married a believer and lives with a man who lost the belief and never said so. The Hendersons are the family whose daughter died — the night the praying became, to David, a performance for an audience of grief and not a transmission to anyone listening. The congregation is four hundred strong at Easter, the overflow room and the folding chairs in the fellowship hall full — the people who need him to believe the thing he no longer does.

The Seer sits in the back pew — the figure who names the Easter sermon, word for word, before David has written it, and the doubt it will cost him.

The warned choice.

Loss A: climb into the pulpit and preach the resurrection he does not believe — keep the wife, the congregation, the job, the house, the architecture — and become, permanently, a man whose entire life is a performance for an audience that needs the performance to be real.

Loss B: tell the truth. Step out of the pulpit, tell Sarah first, then the congregation — topple the first domino and watch them all fall: the marriage strained, the job gone, the house, the standing in the town. Become an honest man with nothing the honesty left standing.

The cruelty is that the lie is also a kindness — four hundred people are held up, on the hardest Sundays, by a faith the pastor performs. The Seer does not tell David whether to believe. The Seer tells him what continuing to stand up there, after the belief has gone, will cost the man inside the robe.

The outcome.

Pyrrhic survival. David survives the year, the architecture mostly intact — but the survival is the cost: the man who keeps the pulpit keeps it by performing the thing he lost, and the performance becomes the life. He learns that doubt, kept secret, does not end a life loudly; it hollows it quietly, from the steeple down, while everyone in the pews believes the building is sound.

How it touches the other books.

First Baptist of Harmon stands on Bishop Hill, the German-American East Side neighborhood that anchors The Good Doctor — near the community hall, the bakery, and Cat Brennan’s house on Vine Street. A pastor visiting St. Clare’s threads David into the hospital books’ world of deathbeds and the doctors who work them — the same rooms where Cat Brennan and Dr. Alvarez do their work.
