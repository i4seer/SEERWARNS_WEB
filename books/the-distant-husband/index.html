The Distant Husband.

James Ota is a graphic designer who has been a good husband for fifteen years, and a caregiver for one, since the diagnosis turned his wife Mei from a partner into a patient who forgets his name on the bad days. At the Main Street florist, where Margaret has sold flowers for forty years, a stranger tells James about Lauren, from the caregiver support group, who understands the specific loneliness of loving someone who is disappearing. She names the coffee that becomes dinner, the motel off Route 9, and the date Mei dies, March, and where James will be when the call comes.

The cast.

James Ota is a graphic designer, a quiet man who builds other people’s logos and has kept his own marriage tidy. Mei, his wife, has an early-onset degenerative diagnosis; the vows James made at thirty have become the contract he honors at forty-five, while the woman who made them with him forgets his name on the bad days and remembers it on the good ones, and the good ones grow rarer. Lauren, from the caregiver support group, has a husband with the same diagnosis, the one person who understands the loneliness of loving someone who is vanishing in front of you.

Margaret, the Main Street florist, gray braid, reading glasses on a chain, forty years behind the counter (the same florist David Marsh buys white carnations from in The Good Father), is the Seer’s vessel here. She tells James, over the flowers, the shape of what loyalty is going to cost.

The Seer scene.

At the florist, Margaret names it, the coffee with Lauren that becomes dinner, the dinner that becomes the thing he tells no one, the motel off Route 9, and the specific cruelty of where he will be when the call comes:

“Mei dies in March. You’ll be at the motel with Lauren when the call comes. You’ll carry that, the timing, the specific cruelty of where you were when your wife died alone, for the rest of your life.”

The warned choice.

Loss A: let it happen. Take the comfort Lauren offers, the understanding, the relief, the brief restoration of being a person and not only a caregiver, and be at the motel, off Route 9, in March, when Mei dies alone.

Loss B: refuse the comfort. Stay home, stay loyal in the narrow, exhausting, unwitnessed way that being a good husband to a disappearing wife requires, and carry the loneliness uncut, the love unmet, with no one who understands.

The book is unsparing about the trap: Lauren is not a villain and the comfort is not nothing. James has been good for years, and the warning is what a good man discovers about himself when the goodness has cost more than he had, that he is capable of the thing he has judged other men for.

The outcome.

Complete collapse, of the self-image, not the marriage, which the disease was already ending. The Seer’s prophecy lands exactly: Mei in March, James at the motel, the call. He survives, and keeps the marriage “tidy” to the end on paper, but he carries the timing for the rest of his life, the good husband who learns that loyalty and honesty stopped being the same thing somewhere in the long middle of a wife’s forgetting.

How it touches the other books.

Margaret the florist is a shared Main Street fixture, her cameo in The Good Father (the white carnations David buys in the good months) predates her turn here as the Seer’s vessel. The motel off Route 9 sits on the same local road that runs west toward Marl and the eldercare facilities and east toward Hartwick, the road the whole series drives.
