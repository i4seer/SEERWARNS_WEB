The Distant Man.

Frank Morrow built four hours of distance between himself and Harmon — the town, the family, the weight of being needed — and called his mother twice a year, Christmas and her birthday, the two calls that let him believe he was still a son. Then his brother Daniel says the word stroke, and the distance collapses into a single drive back. In the St. Clare’s ICU corridor, a stranger tells Frank what the next ten days will cost — the vigil, the decisions, the funeral, and the conversation with the brother who stayed. The distance was supposed to be far enough. It was never far enough.

The cast.

Frank Morrow is the son who left — who put four hours of road between himself and the specific weight of being needed, and who measured his sonhood in two phone calls a year. Eleanor Morrow, seventy-six, is the mother now on the machines in the St. Clare’s ICU, the stroke three days old. Daniel is Frank’s brother — the child who stayed, who lives twenty minutes from the hospital, who has managed their mother’s life and decline and now her death alone, without complaint, with the competence of the one who never left.

Eleanor’s palliative attending is Dr. Patricia Alvarez, the recurring St. Clare’s physician whose work is the accompanying, not the saving; her room card reads MORROW, E. / ALVAREZ. The book is the ten days between the corridor and the grave — and the twenty-year conversation with Daniel underneath them, the one where the brother who stayed asks the brother who left whether the distance was worth what it cost.

The Seer is a man in the ICU corridor — the calm figure in the place between the elevator and the room — who names the first week of December: the thing that makes this a warning and not just a sad story about a dying mother.

The warned choice.

Loss A: stay distant even here. Do the ten days at arm’s length — sign what needs signing, attend the funeral, avoid the conversation with Daniel one more time, and drive the four hours back having lost the last chance to be a brother and a son, exactly as the corridor named.

Loss B: close the distance. Have the conversation he has avoided for twenty years. Let Daniel ask why he left, and find out whether the distance was worth what it cost — which means surrendering the four hours that have been the whole architecture of his adult self.

The Seer names the first week of December because that is when the distance becomes permanent — the deadline past which the conversation can no longer be had. The warning is not about the mother’s death; it is about the thing the death makes finally, unrepairably late.

The outcome.

Complete collapse — of the fiction that distance was protection. Eleanor transitions to Pine Ridge Senior Care in December and dies there in March, as the Seer foretold; the recovery Frank lets himself imagine does not come. Frank drives the four hours home having learned what the four hours cost: a mother he called twice a year, and a brother he could not, in the end, drive the last twenty feet of corridor toward. The distance was the only thing he ever finished.

How it touches the other books.

One of St. Clare’s four hospital books. Dr. Patricia Alvarez recurs here as Eleanor’s attending, as she does in The Steady Hand, The Tired Mother, and The Merciful Nurse; her Pine Ridge discharge plan echoes the Seer’s “first week of December.” Pine Ridge Senior Care is the canon skilled-nursing facility out Route 9 west — the same road past Lakeview Memory Care, where Helen Marsh waited in The Good Father.
