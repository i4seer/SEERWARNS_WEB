The Good Brother.

A senior property assessor in the county office on the second floor of Town Hall. A brother who has always known a guy. Eleven seconds of keyboard on a Friday afternoon — an assessment adjusted, a tax owed reduced, a favor done that has been the structure of every other favor done for thirty-four years. A stranger on the eastbound Greyhound, two stops past the river, eats an orange in the seat next to Dennis Reilly and names the audit Dennis has not yet been told to expect. Dennis has never found the word that comes before no.

The cast.

Dennis Reilly, 47, is a senior property assessor for the county, headquartered in the second-floor office of Harmon Town Hall. He has been an assessor for nineteen years; the second word in his title was added in 2018. He is the older of the two Reilly brothers and was, for most of their childhood, the one their mother counted on, which is a sentence Dennis can still hear in his mother’s voice if he is quiet enough.

Mark Reilly, the younger, runs a small vendor company that does intermittent business with the plant. He has always known a guy. He has, since he was nineteen, brought every problem of his to Dennis, framed in the language of a small favor.

Marie Reilly, the youngest, lives in the state capital and works for the attorney general’s office in the kind of mid-level position that, in the book, becomes load-bearing on a Thursday in March.

Their mother, eighty-one, still lives in the house on Oak Street where she raised them. She makes Sunday dinner for whichever sons can come. The book does not, ever, take her side.

The Seer is on the eastbound Route 22 Greyhound bus, two stops past the bridge. He is a thin man with a paper bag of oranges, one of which he is eating. He does not look at Dennis when he speaks. He speaks the way someone speaks who has just realized he is going to miss his stop.

The Seer scene.

Chapter seven. The eastbound bus, the seat by the window two rows from the back. The Seer sits down in the aisle seat at the stop after the river, although the rest of the bus is empty. He peels an orange. He speaks:

“The audit lands in August. The eleven seconds of keyboard are in the access log they haven’t pulled yet. Your brother has already told Marie. Marie has already called the attorney general’s office. You testify in November. You testify against him. He never speaks to you again. Your mother lives another four years and never understands why the boys stopped coming to dinner together.”

The Seer eats the orange. He gets off at the next stop, which is not a stop where anyone gets off. The bag of oranges remains on the empty seat next to Dennis until Dennis’s stop in downtown Harmon, where he leaves it.

The warned choice.

Dennis’s warned choice is not whether to do the eleven seconds. The eleven seconds happened, in chapter three, six weeks before the Seer scene. The warned choice is what to do in August, when the access-log query crosses the desk of the regional auditor.

Loss A: testify. Cooperate with the AG’s office. Tell the truth about the eleven seconds, and about the other adjustments, smaller and older, that Marie has not yet found but that the auditors will find by January. Lose the brother. Keep the rest.

Loss B: protect Mark. Volunteer nothing. Hire the lawyer the firm in the city has recommended. Let the case build slowly, against both brothers, with everyone’s career as the collateral. Be, at last, the older brother their mother has always asked Dennis to be.

Dennis picks Loss A. The book’s difficulty is that he picks it slowly, over four months, and that the picking is, for most of those months, indistinguishable from indecision. The book is the first in the series to give serious narrative time to the question of how a decision arrives in a person who has spent his life avoiding deciding.

The outcome.

Partial collapse. Dennis testifies. He keeps his job — the office is, in the end, more interested in the larger pattern than in the individual eleven seconds. Mark takes a plea. Mark serves fourteen months in a state facility downstate. Mark does not speak to Dennis again, ever, including on the day of their mother’s funeral in 2031. The mother dies, as the Seer named, four years on, of an unrelated cause, never having understood. Marie and Dennis speak, carefully, twice a year. The book ends with a Sunday dinner Marie cooks at the Oak Street house, after the mother is gone, with two place settings instead of four.
