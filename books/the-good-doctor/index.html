The Good Doctor.

A trauma surgeon and ER physician at St. Clare’s. A teenage son who, by every available metric, is too stable for the year his father has been gone. A mother in slow decline at the facility two towns over. A standing order Cat Brennan wrote at her husband’s deathbed that, eighteen months later, is the only one in her head still holding a beat. The stranger in the cafeteria at 4:12 a.m. names the October the school calls. The patient she keeps missing is in her own house.

The cast.

Catherine “Cat” Brennan, 46, is a trauma surgeon and ER attending at St. Clare’s. She has been at the hospital for eleven years, the last six as the trauma lead. She runs the department in the practical sense: she writes the protocols, she trains the residents, she is the surgeon other surgeons step aside for. She is, in the language of her colleagues, the steadiest hand in the building.

Sam Brennan, her son, is sixteen, a junior at Harmon High. He is by every available metric performing well. He is too well, in a way that, in the book, is a piece of information Cat keeps not noticing because Cat is, by trade, the kind of clinician who responds to instability.

Daniel Brennan, Cat’s husband, died at St. Clare’s eighteen months before the book opens, of a cancer he and Cat both diagnosed early and could not, in the end, outrun. Cat was the physician at his bedside. The last thing he asked of her was a request she has been treating, in her interior life, as a standing order ever since.

Adele Brennan, Cat’s mother, is in slow decline at a facility two towns over. The visits are weekly, scheduled, and on Cat’s calendar in the same color her trauma shifts are.

The Seer is in the basement cafeteria of St. Clare’s at 4:12 a.m. on the third Tuesday of October. He sits down at Cat’s table with a coffee. He does not have a name badge. He is wearing scrubs that almost match the color of the residents’ scrubs and are not the color of any unit’s scrubs.

The Seer scene.

Chapter six. The cafeteria. The table against the wall. Cat has been on shift since 7 p.m. and has, in the past nine hours, lost one patient and saved two and has, at this moment, a granola bar she will not eat. The stranger sits down with a coffee. Cat does not look up. The stranger speaks, in a voice that sounds, the way these voices sound, like a voice Cat has heard before:

“The patient you keep missing is in your own house. You will see it in October, when the school calls. By then the standing order you wrote for your husband at his deathbed is the only thing in your head still holding a beat. You stop being a surgeon for fourteen months. You come back. He does not come back the way you went looking for him — but he comes back.”

The stranger gets up before Cat looks at him. He leaves a folded Sentinel on the table, the high-school football score circled in brown pencil. The cafeteria fluorescent flickers once. Cat eats the granola bar.

The warned choice.

The warning, by chapter six, is already roughly two weeks late. The school has been about to call for a month. Cat’s warned choice is not whether to hear the warning. The warning lands. Cat hears it.

Loss A: stop. Take the leave. Become, for fourteen months, the patient’s mother instead of the building’s surgeon. Trust that the standing order from her husband’s bedside was the order she has, for eighteen months, been filling against the wrong patient.

Loss B: hold the line. The hospital cannot run without her. The residents will be poorly trained without her. The standing order said, in its operative phrase, keep going, and Cat has, since the deathbed, kept going.

Cat picks Loss A. She is the first protagonist in the series to pick Loss A on the day the warning lands — not after weeks, not after the worse thing has happened, but that afternoon, in the residents’ room, with a one-paragraph email to the chief of medicine. The book treats this as the small extraordinary event it is. The book does not congratulate her. The book understands that she could pick Loss A only because the warning had given her permission to misread the standing order.

The outcome.

Refusal. Cat takes the leave on the Wednesday after the cafeteria. She is at home, for the first time since Daniel’s diagnosis, in a posture of attention rather than emergency. Sam is, in the early weeks, more hostile than she expected and then, in the middle weeks, more present than she had thought possible. The school call comes in November, two weeks early, and is, in the actual event, the smaller version of what the warning had named — a school the Seer’s warning had allowed Cat to be ready for. Sam is in therapy by Thanksgiving. The fourteen months pass. Cat returns to St. Clare’s in January of the year after. Sam comes back, not the way she went looking for him, but back.

The book closes with Cat and Sam in the kitchen on a Saturday morning, the standing order from Daniel’s bedside, at last, reread — in the original sense the standing order was given, which was not keep going but, in the sentence that came before it that Cat had not, until the leave, allowed herself to hear: keep him.
