The Good Father.

Seer Warns · Book One

“He places the bet anyway.”

A history teacher. A promise to a mother in a memory-care facility on Route 9. A spreadsheet, a sports book, and a HELOC he forged his wife’s name on. A stranger at the Lamplighter diner tells David Marsh, in the patient voice of someone reading a coroner’s report, exactly how this ends — the date the house goes, the Thursday before Thanksgiving when Sarah finds the envelope, the January morning Emma stops returning his calls. David hears the math the way only a history teacher can. He places the bet anyway.

The cast.

David Marsh, 41, teaches American history at Harmon High School. Room 217. His window overlooks the practice field. He has been teaching the same Civil War unit for fourteen years — he can do the lecture on Antietam from memory, the casualty figures, the angle of the cornfield, the way McClellan refused to pursue. He thinks in probabilities the way a Civil War historian thinks in probabilities. He grew up in this town. He has not lived anywhere else.

Sarah Marsh, his wife, is an ER nurse at St. Clare’s Hospital on Cass Street. She works nights twice a week. She is the kind of nurse other nurses defer to. She does not know about the HELOC in the file box marked taxes. She will find it the Thursday before Thanksgiving.

Emma Marsh, 15, is a freshman in honors English. She is the one who notices first — not the money, the breakfast. The two-plate breakfast that used to be a three-plate breakfast. The small absences. The questions her father stops asking.

Cooper Marsh, 9, plays Little League at Elm Street Park. Cooper does not notice anything. Cooper is the reason David keeps thinking he can fix it.

Helen Marsh, David’s mother, is at Lakeview Memory Care, twenty-two minutes out Route 9. Stage two, sliding toward three. The monthly bill is the original wound. David promised his father at the funeral that Helen would not be moved to Pine Ridge Senior Care, which is cheaper and thirty minutes out the same road. The promise is the thing the bet is meant to keep.

The Seer is at the Lamplighter on a Tuesday in mid-March. Middle-aged. Calm. Hands resting on the Formica like things that have been still for a very long time. The Seer is reading the Harmon Sentinel, the sports section out, and does not look up when David sits down at the booth. The Seer is gone before the second coffee.

The setting.

The Good Father is set across the full breadth of Harmon: the North End where David grew up, the East Side where his life is now, the West Edge where the OTB is in the half-tenanted strip mall by the grain elevator. The Lamplighter is the recurring booth. Harmon High is the recurring room. Lakeview Memory Care is the place David goes on Sundays. The Marsh house, 1847 Third Street in the North End, is the thing he loses.

The Seer scene.

Chapter five. The Lamplighter. The third booth on the right. David sits down across from the stranger because the only other open booth is at the back where the bathroom door swings and David has always hated that booth. David orders coffee. The waitress refills the stranger’s cup at the same time without asking. The diner hum hesitates — only David notices — and the Seer speaks without looking up, voice level, the way a coroner reads the room over a body:

“You’ll lose the house in November. The bank’s already started the paperwork they haven’t sent yet. The HELOC you forged Sarah’s name on — she’ll find it the Thursday before Thanksgiving, in the envelope you left in the file box marked taxes. Emma stops returning your calls in January. By February she stops answering Sarah’s. She’ll come back to her mother eventually. She won’t come back to you.”

The Seer says it the way David has heard a hundred history-channel narrators describe a battle. Past tense. Already settled. The Seer does not look at David. The Seer does not threaten. The Seer is reading the box score for the Browns game. When David looks up from his coffee, the booth across from him is empty, the cup is gone, and the folded Sentinel is on the table with the score circled in a brown pencil.

The warned choice.

Loss A: the warning. The house. The Thursday before Thanksgiving. Emma. The slow public collapse of a marriage David has been quietly destroying for eighteen months.

Loss B: stop. Stop placing the bets. Move Helen to Pine Ridge. Break the promise to his father’s ghost. Tell Sarah about the HELOC tonight, while there is still time to refinance. Let the math be the math.

David picks Loss A. Not because he doesn’t hear the warning — the warning is the most specific thing anyone has ever said to him — but because he cannot survive Loss B. Loss B is the death of the version of himself he has carried since the funeral. Loss A is bigger and more visible and more shameful, but Loss A is, in his head, a thing that happens to him. Loss B is a thing he would have to do.

The fracture tell.

The breakfast. The Marsh family eats breakfast together. Sarah, David, Emma, Cooper. Four plates on the table at six-forty in the morning, every weekday, since Emma was nine. The book opens with four plates and tracks the breakfast across thirty-two chapters as the plate count drops. Emma stops eating breakfast. Three plates. David stops cooking. Two plates. Sarah eats standing at the counter because the table has become the room where the silence sits down with you. One plate. The day the house is sold, in the final chapter, the plates are in a box and the table is on the curb and David eats a granola bar in the car.

The outcome.

Complete Collapse. The Good Father is the series’ first and hardest outcome — the warning lands, the warning arrives exactly, the protagonist loses everything the warning named. The house in November. Sarah by Thanksgiving. Emma by January. The teaching job in March, when the gambling becomes a matter of school-board record. David ends the book in a one-bedroom apartment on Oak Street, eating breakfast alone, the file box marked taxes still on the floor of the closet because he never opened it again after Sarah did.

It is the series’ opening because it is the series’ clearest demonstration of the contract. You were warned. You were warned specifically. You did it anyway. The book does not say this is what every life looks like. The book says this is what one life looked like, and the Seer’s job is not to stop it, only to name it.

Voice signature.

The Good Father is told in the prose rhythm of a history teacher who has read too much about cause and effect to mistake either for accident. David’s sentences are long, structured around probability and consequence, with embedded asides that read like classroom digressions. The book’s recurring metaphors come from American battles — Antietam, Gettysburg, the casualty figures, McClellan’s pause. The narrator describes David’s gambling losses the way a textbook describes the second day at Gettysburg: in numbered casualty tables, with attention to where the line broke.

The bet itself was small — six hundred on Pittsburgh against the spread — but six hundred was no longer the size of the bet, it was the size of the position, the way at Antietam the cornfield itself was no longer the size of the cornfield, it was the size of the casualty count, which by midday had grown larger than the field that contained it. David understood this. He had taught this. He sat in the booth and did the math, the way he had done the math the night before, and the math, the way the math always does, came out the same way it came out every other night.
