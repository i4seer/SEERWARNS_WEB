The Good Man.

Marcus Cole came up the way his mother made him: doing homework in the back seat while she cleaned other people’s houses, swearing at fourteen he’d be different. He works the Harmon County Road Commission — the man who keeps the roads passable, who measures the pothole at the edge of the old neighborhood every Saturday because it’s the one injustice he can hold in inches. Then comes a chance to take down a corrupt official with information that is true but incomplete. A stranger in the barbershop names the cost: not his job, not his freedom — the version of himself his mother was proud of.

The cast.

Marcus Cole works for the Harmon County Road Commission — the plow, the patch crew, the public-works machinery that keeps a hollowing-out county passable in a town that has forgotten to thank the people who do it. He reads rooms for where the power sits — who’s facing whom, whose hand is flat on the table, where the decision already lives — and he thinks in roads: is this a repair or a patch, is this structural or cosmetic. His mother cleaned houses for years so he could become someone different; the becoming is the thing the year puts at risk.

The book’s engine is a corrupt official whose theft from the roads is real — and a piece of information Marcus could deploy against him that is true but incomplete, a weapon whose aim is off even though the target deserves to fall. Keisha is the person who reads people the way Marcus reads rooms, and who will hear the careful non-denial from the doorway. His mother is the standard; his child is the question — are you different? — and the smallest, most expensive lie in the book is the answer same.

The Seer is a woman in the barbershop — the Main Street shop where men in Harmon hear the news before their wives do — who names the reporter, the date, and the exact careful sentence Marcus will say.

The Seer scene.

In the barber chair, the fade nearly finished, the radio and the clippers and the room hold, and the woman in the waiting chair names it without raising her voice:

“It won’t cost your career, your marriage, or your freedom. It will cost the version of yourself your mother is proud of — the one she cleaned eleven years of someone else’s house to make. You’ll send the thing that’s true and not the whole truth. You’ll win. Your child will ask if you’re different, and you’ll say same, and that will be the smallest lie you’ve ever told and the most expensive.”

The warned choice.

Loss A: send it. Use the true-but-incomplete information; bring down the official who is genuinely corrupt; win — and become a man who held two truths (he deserves to lose; the leak is wrong) and acted on the one that wins, and so stopped being the man his mother made.

Loss B: don’t. Keep the weapon holstered because the aim is off; play fair; and lose — let the corrupt man go on stealing from the roads Marcus’s mother walked, because losing cleanly is still losing, and the people who need the roads fixed can’t wait for a weapon that is perfectly calibrated.

The cruelty of this one is that both the corruption and the leak are real, and Marcus is good enough to see both. The Seer doesn’t tell him the official is innocent; the official is guilty. The Seer tells him what winning by the wrong means will cost the self that was the whole point.

The fracture tell.

The pothole. Marcus keeps a tape measure in the glove box and, every Saturday after the barbershop, kneels on the shoulder at the edge of the old neighborhood and measures the depth of the pothole his mother drove over his whole childhood — the injustice he came into public works to fix, expressed in the one unit he trusts: linear inches. The county cold-patches it every spring; it fails every winter; it returns a little deeper. When he finally gets it permanently repaved — with capital bought by the compromise — the reading goes to zero, and the smooth road is the horror: the one promise he kept cleanly, kept by the means that cost him himself.

The outcome.

Pyrrhic survival. Marcus wins, and the roads get fixed, and the pothole goes to zero. He keeps the job, the family, the freedom. What he loses is the man his mother was proud of — the most ambiguous victory in the series, a good man who discovers that being good was never the same as being safe, and that the distance between the smooth road he drew as a boy and the smooth road he finally paved is the distance between who he meant to be and who he became.

How it touches the other books.

Marcus’s public-works world is the Harmon County Road Commission garage on Depot Street — the same county machinery whose careful record-keepers (Dennis Reilly in the assessor’s office, Ruth Calloway in the clerk’s) the series keeps returning to. The barbershop is the Main Street fixture the bible threads through the downtown row, and the theme of a true-but-incomplete weapon rhymes with the honest-record books on either side of it.
