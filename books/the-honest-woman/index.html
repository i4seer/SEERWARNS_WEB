The Honest Woman.

Nineteen years in the basement office of Harmon Town Hall. A binder of water reports she has countersigned without reading because she did not want to know what she was countersigning. Six hundred and forty-two jobs hang on the plant. The woman at the license bureau — the booth in Lake Harmon — tells Ruth Calloway what filing the truth in the May report will cost: the husband, the sister, the mayor, the friends she has shared a parish hall with for twenty years. She files it anyway.

The cast.

Ruth Calloway, 52, is the town clerk of Harmon. Her office is in the basement of Town Hall, four steps down from the lobby, with two windows that look out at sidewalk-level. She has held the position for nineteen years. She is not a Calloway in the founding sense — her maiden name was Pohlman — but the surname has, over the decades, made it easier for her to do her job without anyone asking too many questions about whose side she is on.

Tom Calloway, her husband, is a maintenance foreman at the plant. He has worked there for twenty-six years. He is on the list of foremen the plant is considering cutting in March, which the plant has not yet told him and which the Seer is about to.

Mae Pohlman, Ruth’s younger sister, runs a small accounting practice on the lake side. She is the person Ruth calls, first, with the news Ruth carries home from the license bureau.

The mayor, Dan Hribar, has been mayor for fourteen years. He is the person who first asked Ruth, in her second month on the job, to countersign a report she had not read. He has asked her, every month since, with such a regularity that the asking stopped, decades ago, being a request.

The Seer is at the license bureau, the third teller window, on a Tuesday morning in late February. She is a small woman with a binder of her own. She does not speak until Ruth slides her renewal form across the counter.

The Seer scene.

Chapter four. The license bureau. The third teller window. The Seer slides the form back without stamping it and reads aloud, in the voice of someone reciting a form letter:

“The plant closes in the spring after next. Six hundred and forty-two jobs. The town survives. Your husband’s does not — he’s on the list of foremen they cut in March. You file the report on Tuesday. By Friday the mayor has stopped speaking to you. Your sister calls you in May to ask if it was worth it. You will not have an answer for her until November.”

The Seer then stamps the form. The form is correctly stamped. Ruth carries it out of the bureau without looking at it. The fluorescent over the third teller window flickers once, behind her, as the door closes.

The warned choice.

Loss A: file the May report as the report actually is. Tom on the foreman list anyway. The mayor’s twenty-year silence. Mae’s May phone call. Six months of November before the November phone call.

Loss B: file the May report as she has filed it for nineteen years. Keep the surface of her life. Live, for the rest of it, in the room where she did not file the truth on the day she could have. Watch the river do, slowly, what the river has been doing for twenty years.

Ruth picks Loss A. The book does not present this as moral courage in the heroic sense. The book presents it as the action of a woman who has finally, in her fifty-second year, run out of the energy required to maintain Loss B. The book is, in this way, the inverse of book one: David Marsh cannot survive Loss B because Loss B is the death of a self he has been protecting; Ruth Calloway cannot survive Loss B because Loss B is the slow death of one she has been waiting, without knowing it, to be done protecting.

The outcome.

Partial collapse. Ruth files the report. The state EPA opens an investigation in April. The plant negotiates a remediation order in August that does not, in the end, close the plant — the town survives, exactly as the Seer named. Tom is on the foreman list and is cut in March, as the Seer named, but he is, by the time the cut happens, no longer Ruth’s husband in any operative sense. The mayor stops speaking to her on the Friday after she files. Mae calls in May. The November phone call comes when Mae has, by then, learned to listen for an answer Ruth is finally able to give.

The book ends in the same basement office, in November, with Ruth countersigning the report for that month. The report is now, for the first time in twenty years, the report she is countersigning.
