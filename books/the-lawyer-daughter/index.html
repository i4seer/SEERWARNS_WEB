The Lawyer Daughter.

Diana Torres is the one who holds everything together — the executor, the planner, the one who calls everyone on their birthday. Her father Ernesto was her hero, the immigrant who built Torres Construction from nothing and a brick house on Riverside Avenue in the North End. Six weeks after his funeral, in the estate cleanout, she finds a locked drawer: forty years of $1,200 monthly checks to a woman named Carmen Reyes, in Bridgeport, five hours north through the lake tunnel. A stranger at the funeral reception tells her that whichever she chooses — honor the secret or expose it — she will lose the family she has spent her whole life trying to hold together.

The cast.

Diana Torres, forty-three, is a corporate attorney — mergers and acquisitions for a firm on Washington Street near the courthouse. She is the daughter who left Harmon and succeeded, the “successful one,” which carries its own guilt: she wasn’t here when her father got sick. Married to Marcus, an architect, with two children, Sofia and Mateo, she has built her entire identity on being the dutiful daughter, the keeper of the family’s shape.

Ernesto Torres, her late father, was a Mexican-American immigrant who founded Torres Construction and built much of the North End block by block; his framed first dollar is still on the office wall. Rosa Torres, his widow, still lives in the brick house on Riverside Avenue, in the kitchen where the enchilada recipe lives. Carmen Reyes is the name on the checks — a woman in Bridgeport, the secret Ernesto carried five hours north for forty years. Her daughter Luz teaches English at Bridgeport High — Diana’s half-sister, abandoned, born thirteen months after Diana.

The Seer appears at the funeral reception — among the casseroles and the parish faces, a calm stranger who names, before Diana has even opened the drawer, the shape of the choice already waiting in it.

The drawer.

The locked drawer in Ernesto’s desk holds the cancelled checks — forty years, $1,200 a month, four hundred and eighty of them, to Carmen Reyes in Bridgeport. Diana’s first instinct is the one her whole life has trained: protect. Protect Rosa. Protect the family name. Protect the version of her father everyone believes in. The book is the long unwinding of that instinct — and the drive that proves it cannot hold.

The drive.

The road north is load-bearing. It was Ernesto’s monthly secret journey for forty years — four hundred and eighty trips, Route 11 north, down through the Harmon lake tunnel under the water, five hours into pine-and-birch country. Diana makes the drive once, to find Carmen, and in making it she travels the exact distance her father kept secret — the geography of the lie become the geography of the reckoning.

The warned choice.

Loss A: honor the secret. Keep the drawer closed, protect Rosa from the knowledge, keep the legacy of the man who built the North End — and abandon, a second time, the half-sister he abandoned the first.

Loss B: expose the truth. Tell Rosa, find Luz, set the record straight — and detonate the only thing Diana has ever been, the daughter who held the family together, by being the one who finally broke it open.

The Seer’s warning is the series’ cruelest symmetry: there is no choice that keeps the family. Diana cannot protect her way out, because the family she is protecting is already two families, and has been for forty years. Whichever drawer she leaves open, one of them closes.

The outcome.

Pyrrhic. Diana chooses the truth — drives north, finds Carmen and Luz, brings the second family into the light — and pays the price the stranger named at the reception. The family she held together is not the family she ends with. What she gains is a sister; what she loses is the unbroken version of her father, and the role she had organized her whole life around. The good daughter learns that the dutiful thing and the honest thing were never the same errand, and that her father drove five hours each way, four hundred and eighty times, to keep them apart.

How it touches the other books.

The Torres family is a North End family, and the book pulls the town’s threads through the funeral and the estate: Dennis Reilly handles the probate valuation; Maggie Caldwell (The Loyal Daughter) is at the funeral; Karen Silvestri, the Harmon High principal, sits on the Torres scholarship committee; Maria Gutierrez, the county forensic accountant, is Mrs. Gutierrez’s niece; and Donna pours the coffee at the Lamplighter, as she has for thirty years.
