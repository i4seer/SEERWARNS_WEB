The Quiet Man.

Haskell Hardware, founded 1961. The store Boyd’s father built. The pencil mark on the door frame between the front and the office — four-foot-eleven, in a hand that is not Boyd’s — is his dead son’s last height. The HomePlus circular comes in the mail in March. The stranger at the booth tells Boyd, at the Lamplighter the next morning, the August the store closes and the May his wife leaves. Closing it means closing the last room where Tyler existed.

The cast.

Boyd Haskell, 58, owns and runs Haskell Hardware, the store his father founded in 1961 in the building that had previously been a feed store. He has worked behind the counter for thirty-six years. He knows where everything is by feel. He is one of the four people in Harmon who can quote, from memory, the federal-spec thread count for any common pipe fitting.

Jean Haskell, his wife, retired three years ago from the elementary school where she was the office secretary for twenty-eight years. She has spent the three years since asking Boyd a question. She has, between asking, planted a perennial bed in the side yard that has, this year, finally come in.

Tyler Haskell, their son, died at eleven, twenty-three years ago, on a Wednesday in October. The book does not narrate the death. The book narrates the door frame.

The HomePlus opening is the antagonist of The Quiet Man in the sense that the antagonist of a literary novel is the thing the protagonist has known about for a long time and the protagonist’s wife has known about longer.

The Seer is at the Lamplighter at six-forty in the morning, in the booth Boyd has taken every weekday morning for sixteen years. She is reading a copy of the same HomePlus circular Boyd brought with him in his coat pocket. She does not turn her head.

The Seer scene.

Chapter six. The Lamplighter. The booth Boyd usually takes is taken, so Boyd sits in the one next to it. The partition is about chest height. The Seer speaks across it, without turning, in the voice of someone reading aloud from a circular she is holding flat on the table:

“The store closes the August after next. You hold on twenty-one months past when you should have let go. Jean moves to her sister’s in May of that year. She comes back twice and leaves twice. The pencil mark on the door frame stays where it is. The store has been closed for two years and you still go to it on Saturdays.”

Boyd’s coffee arrives. Boyd looks down at his coffee. When Boyd looks up, the booth across the partition is empty, the HomePlus circular is on the seat, and the grand-opening insert has the date marked through with a heavy brown pencil.

The warned choice.

Loss A: hold on. Twenty-one months of slow attrition. The conversation with Jean that has been waiting since 2004. The May. The Saturdays after.

Loss B: close the store now. In April, the month after the circular. Take the pencil mark off the door frame. Move the door frame, if necessary, into the garage. Survive the loss of the room Tyler is still in.

Boyd picks Loss A, and the book is unsparing about why: Boyd cannot survive Loss B because Loss B is the death of the last room in which Tyler exists. Loss A is, in his interior accounting, a thing that happens to him. Loss B is a thing he would have to do. The series’ first book made this distinction structural; The Quiet Man makes it personal in a way the first could not, because The Quiet Man permits Boyd, in the closing chapters, to know what he is doing while he is doing it.

The outcome.

Complete collapse. The store closes in the August the Seer named. The wife leaves in the May the Seer named — comes back twice, leaves twice. By the book’s closing chapter, two years after the closure, Boyd unlocks the front door of the store every Saturday morning at six-forty, sits in the office with the door frame, and waits for the room to be the room it was. The room remains the room it is. The pencil mark is still at four-foot-eleven. Boyd’s grief is, by now, the room’s only weather.
