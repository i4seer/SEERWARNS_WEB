Map · Establishments · Haskell Hardware

An establishment in Harmon

Haskell Hardware.

The pencil mark on the door frame is at four-foot-eleven, in a hand that is not Boyd’s. Boyd has not repainted that door frame in twenty-three years.

— The Quiet Man, chapter two

The store.

Haskell Hardware is on the west side of Main Street, between the bank and the Methodist church, two doors south of Roasters. Boyd’s father founded it in 1961 in a building that had previously been a feed store and, before that, a livery stable. The original tin ceiling is still up there, painted over six or seven times. The floorboards are the originals; they squeak in patterns the staff can navigate by, in the dark, after closing.

The store sells what hardware stores sell: pipe fittings, single screws by the bin, three brands of paint, the kind of small specific thing a contractor will drive past two big-box stores to acquire because the staff at Haskell will, in fact, know exactly which one. Boyd’s margin on most items is lower than the chain stores’. He absorbs the difference, in the book’s accounting, as the cost of remaining the person his father wanted him to be.

The door frame.

The door frame between the front of the store and the small office at the back has a pencil mark at four-foot-eleven. Tyler Haskell, Boyd’s son, made it the day he became four-foot-eleven, which was a Saturday in the spring of his eleventh year. Tyler died on a Wednesday in October, six months later, in a way the book is careful not to dramatize. The mark has been there for twenty-three years. Boyd has not repainted that door frame. He has repainted the rest of the office twice.

To close the store is, in The Quiet Man’s arithmetic, to close the last room where the mark is. The HomePlus that opens out by the interstate exit in the spring of the book is the thing the store cannot survive, and Boyd has known this since the announcement. The book is about the twenty-one months between knowing and closing, during which Boyd’s wife Jean keeps asking him a question he has answered a different way every time she has asked.

The warning.

Boyd’s Seer scene is at the Lamplighter, the morning after the HomePlus circular comes in the mail with the dates. The Seer is sitting in the booth Boyd usually takes. Boyd takes the booth next to it. The Seer speaks across the partition without turning her head. The warning is verbatim on the Lamplighter’s encounter list.
