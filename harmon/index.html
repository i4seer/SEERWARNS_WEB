Map · Harmon

The town

Harmon.

Unincorporated. Somewhere in the American Midwest. On a county road that does not appear on most state highway maps. Population somewhere around forty-one hundred and dropping. The plant on Cass Street still runs three shifts. The diner has been open since 1977.

What it is.

Harmon is an unincorporated community in a county that does not exist, in a corner of the country that does. The county road that runs through it — the road the residents call Main Street, though that is not its official designation — was paved in 1948 and resurfaced in 1976 and patched, by the township, every spring since. The town sits between two real things: a state route to the east, an interstate to the west, neither of which it is close enough to benefit from.

Its population in the most recent census was 4,138, which is less than it was at the previous census, which was less than the one before. The high school’s enrollment is the bellwether: it loses about a classroom a year. The plant on Cass Street — Calloway-Harmon — still employs roughly six hundred people across three shifts, which is the load-bearing economic fact of the town and the through-line, in The Honest Woman, of the central decision.

The shape of it.

The town is built around three districts the residents do not call districts. Downtown is the three blocks of Main Street where the diner is, where the hardware store is, where the courthouse and the town hall and the post office are. North End is the residential blocks above Main, where the older Victorians are, where the Marshes live, where Helen Marsh taught piano for forty-three years. Lake Harmon is across the causeway — the smaller, older settlement that predates the town proper, the half-island where the dock is, where the marina sits at dusk in book seven.

The Harmon River, which is not a river but a fattened creek, runs along the west edge and feeds the lake. The plant sits at the south end on Cass Street, where the river is wide enough to absorb whatever the plant is permitted to release. Whether what the plant is permitted to release is what it is actually releasing is the question Ruth Calloway spends nineteen years not asking.

What the town is in the books.

Harmon is not a backdrop. The books are not set in Harmon the way a play is set on a stage. The town is one of the protagonists — the protagonist that does not get a name in the eyebrow but appears in every chapter. Every Seer scene happens in a room that is a room of Harmon: the third booth at the Lamplighter, the third teller window at the license bureau, the eastbound bus at the stop before the bridge, the parking lot of the marina at dusk.

The books do not romanticize the town. The town is not picturesque. The town is the place these particular people are going to live in for the duration of these particular stories — and the place the warned choice has to be made, in, against, and through.

What it is not.

Harmon is not a real town. The county is not a real county. The plant on Cass Street has never existed. Readers who write to the imprint claiming to be from Harmon, or to know someone from Harmon, or to have driven through Harmon on the way to somewhere, are read with interest and filed without reply. The town exists where it has always existed, which is in the booth across from you, the moment you sit down.
