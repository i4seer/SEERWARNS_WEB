Seer Warns.

“You were warned.”

A stranger sits down across from you in a diner booth. Hands that have been still for a long time. The voice is patient, flat, the voice of someone reading a document — the dates, the dollar amounts, the room where the worst of it happens. You believe every word. You do it anyway. Because the cost of stopping is something you can’t pay either.

The pattern.

One stranger. Twenty warned choices. Read in any order.

The Seer’s warning is never wrong. It is never threatening. It is never withheld. It arrives at the moment the protagonist has already committed — placed the bet, signed the contract, opened the drawer — and it describes, with the patient precision of a coroner, exactly what comes next.

The protagonist hears the warning. The protagonist believes the warning. The protagonist continues anyway. Not from stupidity. Not from arrogance. From the recognition that the cost of stopping is its own kind of death — and the warned trajectory, at least, is a trajectory they can walk.

Each book is a standalone novel. Pick the hook that lands hardest and start there. But every book takes place in the same town, on the same shared map, in the same two-year window. The history teacher in The Good Father walks past the town clerk from The Honest Woman on the courthouse steps. The hardware store closes. The plant closes. The lake stays where it is.
