Map · People · Boyd Haskell

Protagonist · The Quiet Man

Boyd Haskell.

Fifty-eight. Owner of Haskell Hardware, the store his father built in 1961. Thirty-six years behind the counter; he knows where everything is by feel. One of four people in Harmon who can quote the federal-spec thread count for any common pipe fitting from memory.

Who he is.

Boyd’s father, Dale Haskell, founded the store in 1961 in a building that had been a feed store and, before that, a livery stable. Boyd has run it for thirty-six years. His margins are lower than the chains’; he absorbs the difference, in the book’s accounting, as the cost of remaining the person his father wanted him to be. He lives out on Mill Creek Road, a modest older road of small houses at the edge of town, with his wife Jean.

The wound.

The door frame between the front of the store and the small office at the back has a pencil mark at four-foot-eleven, in a hand that is not Boyd’s. Tyler Haskell, Boyd’s son, made it the day he became four-foot-eleven, a Saturday in the spring of his eleventh year. Tyler died six months later, on a Wednesday in October. The mark has been there twenty-three years. Boyd has repainted the rest of the office twice and never touched that door frame. The store is the last room where the mark is — the last room where Tyler, in any sense, still exists.

The wife.

Jean Haskell retired three years ago from the elementary school where she was the office secretary for twenty-eight years. She has spent the three years since asking Boyd a question — the same question, answered a different way each time — about when he will let the store go. Between askings she planted a perennial bed in the side yard that has, this year, finally come in.

The warning.

The HomePlus circular comes in the mail in March, the grand-opening date on the insert. The next morning Boyd’s usual booth at the Lamplighter is taken — by the Seer, a woman reading the same circular, flat on the table. Boyd takes the booth next to it. She speaks across the partition without turning her head:

“The store closes the August after next. You hold on twenty-one months past when you should have let go. Jean moves to her sister’s in May of that year. She comes back twice and leaves twice. The pencil mark on the door frame stays where it is. The store has been closed for two years and you still go to it on Saturdays.”

Boyd looks down at his coffee. When he looks up, the booth is empty and the circular is on the seat, the date marked through with a heavy brown pencil.

What he loses.

The store, in the August the Seer named. Jean, in the May — she comes back twice and leaves twice, exactly as warned. He chooses to hold on not because he doesn’t believe the warning — it is the most specific thing anyone has said to him — but because closing the store is a thing he would have to do, and losing it slowly is a thing that happens to him, and only the second kind of loss is survivable. By the closing chapter, two years after the closure, Boyd unlocks the front door every Saturday at six-forty, sits in the office with the door frame, and waits for the room to be the room it was.

Where else he appears.

Haskell Hardware sells to the whole town, so Boyd’s counter is a crossroads. Dennis Reilly comes in for a tape measure with no kink in the blade (The Loyal Man); Maggie Caldwell comes in for structural screws and the news that the chain is quoting her crews direct (The Loyal Daughter). The Haskells turn out for every youth team in The Good Father, and Cooper Marsh stays at their house. The store is “Boyd-run” in every book up to his own — an open Main Street fixture whose fate is the one thing the series saves for The Quiet Man.
