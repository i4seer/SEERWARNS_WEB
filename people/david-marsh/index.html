Map · People · David Marsh

Protagonist · The Good Father

David Marsh.

Forty-one. History teacher. Room 217 at Harmon High. Born in this town. Has not lived anywhere else. Hears the math the way only a history teacher can — and places the bet anyway.

Who he is.

David Marsh has taught American history at Harmon High School for fourteen years. The window of room 217 looks out on the practice field. He can deliver the Antietam lecture from memory — the casualty figures, the angle of the cornfield, the way McClellan refused to pursue an army that was already broken. He is, by trade, the man in this town most likely to recognize a situation that is already lost. He is, also, the protagonist of The Good Father.

He grew up on Maple Street, three houses down from where he and Sarah live now. He went to a small liberal-arts college a few hours south on a partial scholarship, came home for his father’s funeral in the summer after his junior year, and never quite left again. He met Sarah at St. Clare’s the night his father came in, which is a piece of family history he and Sarah have polished into a story they tell at dinner parties. There are no dinner parties anymore.

The wound.

His mother Helen is in Lakeview Memory Care, twenty-two minutes out Route 9. The monthly bill is the original wound. At his father’s graveside David promised, out loud, with witnesses, that Helen would not be moved to Pine Ridge Senior Care — cheaper, thirty minutes out the same road, the place where his father’s cousin died of being misplaced. The promise is the load-bearing thing. Everything else in David’s adult life is what the promise is sitting on top of.

The gambling, in this reading, is not a vice. It is a math problem. Lakeview costs more than the household earns. The math has a single open variable, and David is the kind of man who, when he sees an open variable, cannot stop trying to solve it.

The warning.

Chapter five. The Lamplighter, the third booth on the right. The stranger reads the Sentinel sports section and speaks without looking up:

“You’ll lose the house in November. The bank’s already started the paperwork they haven’t sent yet. The HELOC you forged Sarah’s name on — she’ll find it the Thursday before Thanksgiving, in the envelope you left in the file box marked taxes. Emma stops returning your calls in January.”

David hears it the way only a history teacher can — in past tense, settled, a battle already lost on a field he is still walking across. He goes home and places the bet anyway, because Loss B — calling Pine Ridge, breaking the graveside promise — is the death of the version of himself he has been carrying since the funeral. Loss A is bigger. Loss A is also, in the grammar of David’s head, a thing that happens to him, which is the only kind of loss he can survive.

What he loses.

The house, on the Tuesday before Thanksgiving, three days early. Sarah, in the kitchen, with the envelope from the file box, on the Thursday the Seer named. Emma, in January, the way the Seer named. The teaching job, in March, when the gambling becomes a matter of school-board record. The version of himself he came into the booth with — in the booth, before he stood up.

Where else he appears.

David is on the periphery of three other books. In The Honest Woman he is the parent at the back of the auditorium at the school-board hearing — not named, not central, but recognizable to readers of book one. In The Good Doctor he is a patient: an outpatient psych referral Cat Brennan never quite places. In The Quiet Man he is, briefly, a man buying a single replacement bracket at Haskell Hardware on a Saturday morning in the August the store closes.
