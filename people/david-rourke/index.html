Map · People · David Rourke

Protagonist · The Believer

David Rourke.

Pastor of First Baptist of Harmon for sixteen years, on Bishop Hill. The steeple has been the same steeple longer than the faith was ever there.

Who he is.

David has stood in the white-steepled pulpit on Bishop Hill for sixteen years — the shepherd of a congregation that swells to four hundred at Easter. He is good at the work; the work is holding people up.

The wound.

Three years ago, sitting with the Hendersons while their nine-year-old daughter died of leukemia, David prayed and heard only a man talking to a ceiling — and kept talking, because the parents needed it. The faith has been gone ever since. He has told no one, not even his wife Sarah, because telling would topple the first domino: wife, congregation, job, house, the whole architecture of a life.

The warning.

A stranger in the back pew names the Easter sermon, word for word, before David has written it — the literal resurrection, the empty tomb, the stone rolled away — and that he won’t believe a word of it, and will say it anyway, because four hundred people need him to.

What he keeps, and loses.

David climbs into the pulpit and preaches the resurrection he does not believe. He keeps the marriage, the congregation, the job, the architecture — and that survival is the cost: the man who keeps the pulpit keeps it by performing the thing he lost, and the performance becomes the life. Doubt, kept secret, hollows a life quietly, from the steeple down.

Where he fits.

First Baptist stands on Bishop Hill, the East Side neighborhood of The Good Doctor; a pastor visiting St. Clare’s threads David into the hospital books’ deathbeds — the rooms where Cat Brennan, Luisa Dominguez, and Dr. Alvarez do their work.
