Map · People · David Rourke

Protagonist · The Silent Pastor

David Rourke.

Pastor of First Baptist of Harmon for twenty-four years, twenty-four notebooks of handwritten sermons in the desk drawer. Somewhere in there the faith went quiet, and he kept preaching from the inventory the delivery left behind.

Who he is.

David has stood in the First Baptist pulpit for twenty-four years, twenty-four notebooks of sermons, the forty-seventh whiter than the rest. He is good at the work, and the work is holding people up. But somewhere in the long middle the river ran dry: the loss of faith arrived not as a crisis but as a quiet structural edit, the fourth point of his sermons, the calling point, the one that required feeling, disappearing in 2021, three years before he, or anyone, noticed.

The wound.

He keeps preaching a faith that is gone, drawing from the inventory the way a store sells stock the supplier has stopped delivering. He has told no one, not his wife Linda, who says at the kitchen table, I can see you’re not in the room anymore. Telling would topple the first domino, because leaving the pulpit is leaving the house, and the town, and the marriage that was built on the pulpit.

The warning.

Between visits to a dying parishioner, Martha Ingram in Room 412, a woman with still hands in the Harmon Clinic waiting room names the shape of what telling the truth will cost, and leaves a church bulletin in his own type with one sentence on the back: you stopped writing four points three years ago, the thing he had not told himself. (The verbatim warning is on the book’s page.)

What he loses, and finds.

Martha dies; her daughter Ellen hears the gap; Linda asks the question at the kitchen table; the deacon Wendell’s board hears the sentence (it is not a season; it is the climate); and David leaves the pulpit, the parsonage, the town, and the marriage for a card table in a Granger apartment and the honest silence. But a year later he moves back; Linda meets him in the gravel driveway and the hug lasts forty-three seconds; and he returns to a second pastorate that pastors the doubt itself, the fourth point restored as the witness paragraph. He loses the faith that was a secret and gains a faith that is a fact. Pyrrhic survival; the carrying continues.

Where he fits.

First Baptist is the series’ pulpit. David sits with the dying on the same St. Clare’s floors where Cat Brennan, Luisa Dominguez, and Dr. Alvarez do their work; the Lamplighter, Donna, and the Granger refuge recur; and he later names Anne Loring and Marcus Cole from the pulpit.
