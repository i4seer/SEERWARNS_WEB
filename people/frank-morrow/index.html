Map · People · Frank Morrow

Protagonist · The Distant Man

Frank Morrow.

The son who left. He built four hours of distance between himself and Harmon — the town, the family, the weight of being needed — and measured his sonhood in two phone calls a year.

Who he is.

Frank put the four hours of road between himself and Harmon on purpose, and called his mother twice a year — Christmas and her birthday — the two calls that let him believe he was still a son. The distance was supposed to be far enough.

The wound.

His mother Eleanor, seventy-six, has had a massive stroke and is on the machines in the St. Clare’s ICU. His brother Daniel — the one who stayed, who lives twenty minutes away, who managed their mother’s whole decline alone — said the word stroke, and the distance collapsed into a single drive back.

The warning.

A stranger in the ICU corridor names the next ten days — the vigil, the decisions, the funeral, the will — and the twenty-year conversation with Daniel underneath them, and the first week of December: the thing that makes this a warning and not just a sad story about a dying mother.

What he loses.

Eleanor transitions to Pine Ridge Senior Care in December and dies there in March, as foretold. Frank drives the four hours home having learned what they cost: a mother he called twice a year, and a brother he could not, in the end, drive the last twenty feet of corridor toward. The distance was the only thing he ever finished.

Where he fits.

One of St. Clare’s hospital books. Eleanor’s palliative attending is Dr. Patricia Alvarez, who recurs in The Good Doctor, The Good Surgeon, and The Good Nurse. Pine Ridge is the eldercare facility out Route 9 west, near Lakeview, where Helen Marsh waited in The Good Father.
