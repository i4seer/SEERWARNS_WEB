Mother · The Good Father

Helen Marsh.

Seventy-four. Lakeview Memory Care, twenty-two minutes out Route 9. The original wound. The reason for the math. The promise the graveside was about.

Who she is.

Helen Marsh, seventy-four, was for forty-three years a piano teacher in a converted parlor at the front of the house on Maple Street, three houses down from where her son David and his family now live. She taught most of the children in North End Harmon, including, at one point or another, half of the parents who still wave at her in the diner. She was the last reliable employer of children in this town before piano became the thing children did briefly and stopped.

Her diagnosis, the early-stage diagnosis, not the late one, arrived in the same week as her husband’s. They both knew. They told the family separately, six months apart, in the parlor where she had taught. The piano is still there. It has been tuned three times since she stopped playing.

Lakeview.

Lakeview Memory Care is a small facility on Route 9, twenty-two minutes from David’s house if there is no construction. It is owned by a regional company headquartered in Indiana. Helen has a room facing the courtyard. Her two pictures, the one of the family at the lake, the one of David in his college robes, are taped to the wall above the bedside table because the family has been told, gently, that frames are a hazard.

The monthly bill is, in the book, a number. The book makes a point of giving the reader the number twice: in chapter two, when David opens the envelope, and in chapter twenty-nine, when David places the last bet. The number does not change between those chapters. Everything else in David’s life does.

The promise.

At his father’s graveside, the August before the book opens, David promised, out loud, with witnesses, with Sarah at his elbow, that Helen would not be moved to Pine Ridge Senior Care, the cheaper facility thirty minutes out the same road. The promise was not for Helen. Helen, in the late diagnosis, will not be aware of the move. The promise was for David’s father, who in his last week kept naming the cousin who had died at a place very much like Pine Ridge, of being misplaced.

The Seer’s warning in chapter five does not name Helen. It does not need to. Helen is the load-bearing object at the bottom of every other thing the warning names, the house, the HELOC, the bet, the Thursday before Thanksgiving. To unname Loss A would be to move Helen. To move Helen would be to break the graveside promise. To break the graveside promise would be Loss B, the unsurvivable loss, the loss David cannot face. So he places the bet.

What she does not know.

The book’s most quietly devastating chapter is the one in which David visits Helen at Lakeview in late October, three weeks before the house goes. She is having a good afternoon. She knows him. She asks him, in the courtyard, with the sun on the maples, whether Sarah is still on nights. He tells her yes. She nods. She says: tell her the rhododendron needs cutting back. She has been saying this for nine years. She will say it once more, in March, two days before she dies, when David is no longer the one bringing her the message.
