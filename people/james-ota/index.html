Protagonist · The Distant Husband

James Ota.

A graphic designer who has been a good husband for fifteen years, and a caregiver for one, since the diagnosis turned his wife from a partner into a patient who forgets his name on the bad days.

Who he is.

James builds other people’s logos and keeps his own marriage tidy. The vows he made at thirty have become the contract he honors at forty-five, while the woman who made them with him, Mei, forgets his name on the bad days and remembers it on the good ones, and the good ones grow rarer.

The wound.

The marriage was, in the way that counts, ending before the book opens, not by choice but by disease. Lauren, from the caregiver support group, has a husband with the same diagnosis; she is the one person who understands the specific loneliness of loving someone who is disappearing in front of you.

The warning.

At the Main Street florist, Margaret, forty years behind the counter, names the coffee with Lauren that becomes dinner, the motel off Route 9, and the date Mei dies (March) and where James will be when the call comes.

What he carries.

The prophecy lands exactly: Mei in March, James at the motel, the call. He survives, and keeps the marriage “tidy” on paper to the end, but carries the timing for the rest of his life, the good man who discovers he is capable of the thing he judged other men for, and learns that loyalty and honesty stopped being the same thing somewhere in the long middle of a wife’s forgetting.

Where he fits.

Margaret the florist is a shared Main Street fixture, her cameo in The Good Father (the carnations David buys in the good months) predates her turn here as the Seer’s vessel.
