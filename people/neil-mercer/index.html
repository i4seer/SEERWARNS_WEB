Map · People · Neil Mercer

Protagonist · The Good Surgeon

Neil Mercer.

Forty-seven. Neurosurgeon at St. Clare’s — the hands the whole hospital trusts, the surgeon other surgeons send the hard cases to. Six months ago, mid-resection, his right hand trembled. He is still operating.

Who he is.

Neil notices hands — it is the first line of his professional identity. Fellowship-trained, board-certified, one of the best in the region. His entire self, since age twelve, lives in his hands and the ritual that prepares them: the amber Betadine scrub, each finger, the gateway that turns a man into a surgeon. His father was a doctor, dead three years, the standard Neil still measures himself against.

The wound.

The tremor. Three seconds, the first time, during a meningioma resection; it has come back — twice more in the OR, once at dinner over a wine glass. He suspects early-onset Parkinson’s. He has not been tested. He has not told his wife Claire, a pediatrician at the same hospital who has quietly begun keeping a timeline of what she has seen. To report the tremor is to stop operating immediately — identity death, in a meeting in the chief’s office.

The warning.

At the cafeteria coffee station, a stranger with placed, waiting hands names the date, the patient (Thomas Yeung, nine), the 1.3-second tremor at the fourth ventricle, and the lie his wife won’t forgive — then leaves a sealed surgical glove, Neil’s size, expiration date the day of the surgery.

“Claire will not leave you over the malpractice. She understands medical error. She’ll leave you over the lie.”

What he loses.

Neil keeps operating. On February 14th, at 11:47, the tremor arrives exactly as named; the boy survives, impaired; the malpractice suit comes; the concealment surfaces; Claire leaves over the lie, not the error. He ends a man whose still hands rest in his lap and whose Betadine soap dries and cracks on the counter, for hands that no longer operate. A complete collapse, sprung by a surgeon who could not stop being a surgeon.

Where else he appears.

One of St. Clare’s four hospital books. Cat Brennan crosses Neil on the skybridge in The Good Doctor — reading the surgeon hiding his tremor, “the patient always says the same word: fine.” Dr. Patricia Alvarez, the palliative attending shared across the hospital books, is called for a case where Neil’s work ends.
