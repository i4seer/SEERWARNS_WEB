Map · People · Sarah Marsh

Recurring · first appears in The Good Father

Sarah Marsh.

ER nurse at St. Clare’s. Two nights a week, plus the Saturday Tom can’t cover. The nurse other nurses defer to. The wife who finds the envelope the Thursday before Thanksgiving.

Who she is.

Sarah Marsh, thirty-nine, has been an emergency-department nurse at St. Clare’s Hospital for seventeen years. She is on the night side: 7 p.m. to 7 a.m., Sunday and Tuesday, plus the Saturday her colleague Tom can’t cover. New residents learn within their first month that Sarah is the person to ask when the senior attending is unavailable and the chart is doing something that does not match the patient. She is small, plainspoken, and unembarrassable. She has held more dying hands than anyone in the building except the chaplain.

She grew up in Lake Harmon, the sister-village across the water, and crossed the causeway every morning to attend Harmon High. She met David in the ER waiting room the night his father came in, a meeting they have polished, over the years, into a story with the worn places of a coin. She is not, herself, a polisher.

What she sees.

Sarah is the book’s second-best reader of the situation, after the Seer. She is not in the Lamplighter booth in chapter five — the warning lands on David, not her — but she is in every chapter that follows, watching. She notices the breakfast first. She notices the rerouted bank statements second. She notices the way David leaves the room when his phone rings third. She does not yet have the word HELOC in her head; she has only the shape it makes in his behavior.

What she does with what she sees, for most of the book, is wait. This is not weakness. Sarah is a triage nurse. Sarah is in the habit of letting a patient declare themselves before she calls the resuscitation team. The book’s slow violence is, in part, the violence of how long that triage takes when the patient is the man you sleep next to.

The Thursday.

The Thursday before Thanksgiving, while David is at school and Cooper is at practice and Emma is at a friend’s, Sarah opens the file box marked taxes. She is looking for the HSA statement for Helen’s November invoice. She finds the HELOC envelope. She reads it twice. She closes the box. She puts the box back on the shelf in the spot it has lived in for nine years. She does not cry. She makes a list, in her head, of which of her family members she will tell, and in what order, and on what day, and then she crosses every name off the list except Emma’s, which she leaves for last.

After.

Sarah is, in the books that follow The Good Father, one of the series’ quiet anchors. She is the nurse who attends Helen Marsh at end-of-life in The Good Doctor. She is the friend Ruth Calloway calls on the night the audit becomes public, in The Honest Woman. She is the wife who, by the time book five opens, lives in the same house she has always lived in — without David — and who has, in the front yard, planted a row of crocuses that come up every March without fail.
