Map · People · Warren Gibbs

Protagonist · The Watchful Teacher

Warren Gibbs.

English teacher at Harmon High for thirty years — the classroom on the floor below David Marsh’s history room, the same books, the one student a decade who makes it worth it.

Who he is.

Thirty years, the same lectures, the same red pen, the rare student who makes the decades worth it. Warren does not know that his thirty-first year is the one where the world changes the rules and the rules he taught by become the rules that end him.

The wound.

The thing that made him a good teacher for thirty years — a willingness to go past the line for a student who needed it — is the thing that ends him. Tyler Brennan’s college-application essay is the line he crosses one more time, between helping and writing.

The warning.

A woman at the back of his own classroom names the essay, the discovery, and the board hearing — the specific humiliation of a thirty-year teacher defending himself to a board half his age, while his wife Margaret sits in the back row and learns, for the first time, the thing he did.

What he loses.

Warren survives the hearing — reduced, reprimanded, the thirty years recast in a single sentence — but the version of himself that was simply a good teacher does not. A career built over three decades ends on a thing too small to explain to the board that ends it.

Where he fits.

Warren’s English room is one floor below David Marsh’s Room 217 at Harmon High — the same building that loses a history teacher to a different unraveling in The Good Father. The surname Brennan ties Tyler lightly to the hospital-book Brennans of The Tired Mother.
